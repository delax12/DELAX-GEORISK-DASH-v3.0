How DELAX GEO-RISK measures geopolitical exposure.

01 · The framework

Risk structures, not headlines

The platform models each geopolitical situation as a risk structure: a defined set of transmission channels (oil, shipping, chip supply, currencies, defense spending, growth), a set of probability-weighted scenarios, and a matrix of sector sensitivities that translate channel stress into expected portfolio impact. The Exposure Score you see anywhere on the platform is your holdings, weighted, run through that matrix — computed in your browser, against the same numbers documented here.

Two structures are live: the Strait of Hormuz / Iran standoff and the Taiwan Strait semiconductor chokepoint. They are deliberately different instruments: Hormuz transmits chiefly through energy and shipping; Taiwan through chip supply and the technology complex, with no oil channel at all. Geopolitical risk is not one trade, and the platform does not model it as one.

02 · The calibration standard

Every number carries its evidence tier

Not all model figures deserve equal confidence, and the platform says so on its face. Every structure carries a visible calibration tier:

TierMeaningApplied to
EMPIRICAL Sensitivities fitted to the structure's own observed eventHormuz / Iran
UNPRICED Analytical estimates anchored to published institutional research; the event has never occurred, so no market response exists to fitTaiwan Strait
DRAFT Unvalidated first pass — never shipped to users—

Three rules govern the tiers. A structure never inherits another structure's tier. A tier can only be upgraded by evidence — an event occurring and being measured. And each structure carries a standing maintenance contract: scheduled reviews (visible as the "Reviewed / next" stamp beside every tier badge), defined re-test triggers, and an append-only revision log.

03 · Hormuz — the empirical record

Fitted to the 2026 war, and validated against it

The Strait of Hormuz was closed by war between 28 February and the ceasefire of 8 April 2026. Brent ran from a $70.9 pre-war average to a peak of $138.2 on 7 April (FRED daily spot) and has since returned to roughly $69. The war reached ceasefire; the crisis has not — transits remain below pre-war levels, war-risk premiums persist, and the model's central case treats the present armed truce, not a hypothetical conflict, as the baseline.

Because the event occurred inside the price history, the model's sector sensitivities are fitted to it directly: 41 instruments, weekly closes 2020–2026, with every sector return measured net of the S&P 500 over identical windows. Market-netting is the load-bearing methodological choice — it strips the concurrent Federal Reserve cycle (2022) and the AI equity cycle (2026) out of the conflict signal. The 2022 Ukraine invasion serves as a secondary calibration event at reduced weight; the 2023–24 Red Sea crisis serves as a falsification test, confirming that oil-sensitive sensitivities do not fire when oil stress is absent.

Validation record

Tested out of sample against the full 2026 event, the current model's mean absolute error is 2.5 percentage points per sector. The preceding version — calibrated on the Ukraine analogue before the Hormuz event could be used — erred by 19.0 points and systematically overstated conflict sensitivity. It was replaced, and the revision is logged.

That replacement reflects the platform's governing discipline: when observation contradicts the model, the model changes. Airline sensitivity was cut 3.6× when measured fuel-cost impact proved milder than assumed; commodity funds mis-grouped with gold were re-classified when their wartime behaviour diverged (+24.5% versus gold's +0.9%); emerging-market sensitivities were re-signed when both 2022 and 2026 showed exporter gains outweighing importer pain. Corrections of this kind are not exceptions to the methodology — they are the methodology.

04 · Taiwan — the unpriced risk

Measured absence of pricing, anchored analysis of impact

No Taiwan blockade has ever occurred. Chip supply has never been interrupted by geopolitics. There is therefore no market response to fit — and rather than disguise that, the platform makes it the structure's central, testable finding.

The pricing evidence: five candidate analogues were tested with the same market-excess method used for Hormuz. The December 2025 PLA escalation — the sharpest in decades — saw the foundry complex rise 20.6% against the market; the AI demand cycle did not merely mute the risk signal, it inverted it. The August 2022 Pelosi episode produced the only coherent fear response on record (foundry −8.3%), and it was small. Physical fab damage (the April 2024 Hualien earthquake) and genuine chip scarcity (2021) left no usable price signature. Each test, with windows and verdicts, is published in the structure's Pricing Evidence panel, and every future escalation is re-tested by a frozen, validated pipeline against a fixed threshold.

The impact model: scenario magnitudes are anchored to Bloomberg Economics (February 2026): a year-long blockade at −5.0% of world GDP; war at −9.6%, some $10.6 trillion in the first year; lagging-edge chip output −35% and −62% respectively. Sector sensitivities are then set analytically from revenue exposure and supply-share reasoning so the model reproduces outcomes consistent with those published figures. They are estimates of a never-observed event — a map, not a forecast — and the platform labels them accordingly everywhere they appear.

05 · Using the platform

Four tools that answer real questions

01 Score your actual portfolio — the Exposure Desk

Open the Exposure Desk, add your holdings (search covers 100+ names; custom tickers can be sector-tagged), pick a structure and scenario, and read your Conflict Exposure Score: the modelled move of your book, decomposed by channel and by holding, worst-hit first. Everything computes in your browser — your portfolio is never sent anywhere.

02 Compare risks — the structure switcher

On the dashboard, switch between HORMUZ and TAIWAN and watch the same portfolio tell two different stories. An oil crisis barely touches a typical tech-heavy book; a Taiwan blockade transforms it. Then use the scenario tabs — each carries its probability, in numbers and plain words — to see the range, not just the base case.

03 Ask your own question — Investor Intelligence

The AI panel on the dashboard answers free-form questions ("How exposed is my portfolio to a Taiwan blockade?") using the live model state — the active structure, scenario, and current data — and answers in a fixed discipline: what changed, why it matters to you, what to watch next. It describes exposure; it does not give buy or sell instructions.

04 Judge the confidence — tiers and evidence

Before acting on any number, check its badge. EMPIRICAL means fitted to an observed event; UNPRICED means analytical and anchored. On the Taiwan view, the Monthly tab holds the full Pricing Evidence ledger — every escalation tested, every verdict, and the running count. The review stamp beside each badge shows when the structure was last examined and when it is due again.

06 · Limits

What this model does not claim

One war is one sample.

The 2026 Hormuz war ran six weeks to ceasefire. A longer closure could transmit through corporate earnings in ways a six-week shock never did. Empirical sensitivities describe what has happened, not every tail that hasn't.

Some channels are judgment-tier, and are labelled.

No free high-frequency series exists for tanker rates, real-time global GDP, or defense appropriations; their stress levels are stated estimates. The emerging-market currency channel is the weakest-evidenced in the model — in both measured conflicts, exporter and importer moves largely offset. Confidence scores on the dashboard's indicators reflect these gradations.

Unpriced means unpriced.

Taiwan figures are anchored estimates of a never-observed event. They are built for exposure comparison and stress-thinking, not point prediction, and the linear model's extreme-scenario outputs should be read as order of magnitude.

This is analysis, not advice.

The platform measures and explains exposure. It does not issue investment recommendations, and nothing on it should be read as a personalised instruction to buy or sell.

Why this page exists

An exposure score you cannot interrogate is a number taken on faith. Everything above can be checked: the events are public, the instruments are liquid, the windows are stated, the anchors are cited, and the method is one line of arithmetic — sector return minus market return. Where the model has been wrong, the correction is on the record. If you believe a sensitivity is still wrong, this page gives you everything needed to make that case.
