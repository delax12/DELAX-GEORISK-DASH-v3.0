What the Exposure Score is doing

Every sector on the Exposure Desk carries a beta for each risk channel — a number describing how much that sector moves when oil, shipping, food, defense spending, global growth, or emerging-market currencies come under stress. Your score is your holdings, weighted, run through those betas.

So the betas are the whole model. If they are guesses, the score is a guess. They are not guesses.

01 · The evidence

The event, not an analogue

On 28 February 2026 a US–Israeli air war against Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz. Iran mined the waterway and struck merchant shipping; traffic fell to almost nothing. Brent ran from $70.9 to a peak of $138.2 on 7 April — the day before the ceasefire. It has since round-tripped to about $69, but the strait has not normalised: transits remain below pre-war, war-risk premiums persist, and vessel attacks continue.

An earlier version of this model treated the 2022 Ukraine invasion as a proxy for a Hormuz event. That was a mistake. The Hormuz event was already sitting in the data.

02 · The method

One choice carries everything

The rule

Every return is measured net of the S&P 500 over the same window.

Sector return minus market return. That's the whole arithmetic.

The first half of 2022 was not only a war. It was the fastest Federal Reserve hiking cycle in four decades. Growth stocks fell hard in that window and most of that fall was about interest rates, not Ukraine. In 2026 the same trick was needed for a different confound: the AI cycle.

A naive calibration absorbs the rate shock into the war beta and produces a model that screams whenever anything happens. Netting each sector against the broad market strips out what the whole market was doing anyway, and leaves the part that belongs to the conflict.

The oil channel is Brent, not WTI.

Hormuz is a seaborne crude shock. In 2026, WTI peaked at $115 while Brent hit $138 — US domestic crude was insulated, and shipped crude was not. In 2022 the two moved almost together, so the distinction never surfaced. Calibrating on the wrong barrel would have inflated every oil sensitivity by roughly 60%.

03 · The headline finding

The old model overstated everything by two to three times.

Both wars delivered almost identical oil stress — Brent peaked at $133 in 2022 and $138 in 2026. Yet sectors moved less than half as far in the real Hormuz war.

Market-excess returns. Same oil shock, roughly half the response.

Why the gap? 2022 caught energy equities after a decade of underinvestment. That +46% was a one-time re-rating as much as an oil pass-through — and a re-rating only happens once. Fitting to it inflates every future call. The new model deliberately does not chase those magnitudes.

Out-of-sample test

Run against the real Hormuz war, the old betas missed by an average of 19.0 percentage points per sector. The new ones miss by 2.5.

04 · What broke

Four sectors were pointing the wrong way.

Correction 01 · Emerging markets

The "EM gets crushed" thesis is refuted by both wars.

The old model predicted EM equity would fall 17.8% on a Hormuz shock. It rose 7.2% — and rose 7.8% in Ukraine too. Two conflicts, same answer, opposite to the model.

The reason is composition: the EM index is commodity exporter-heavy. Importers hurt, exporters win, and the exporters carry the index.

was   em_equity · fx −0.50, gdp −0.35, oil −0.15  → −17.8%
now   em_equity · oil +0.20, gdp −0.15, fx −0.10  → +6.9%
Correction 02 · Utilities

Utilities are a haven, not a victim.

Scored as a mild loser. In the actual war they gained 10.1% over the market — the defensive bid, the same instinct that normally lifts gold. (Gold, meanwhile, managed +0.9%. It did not show up.)

was   utilities · gdp −0.05, cpi −0.20  → −6.0%
now   utilities · gdp +0.35, cpi −0.10  → +4.5%
Correction 03 · Gulf producers

In a Hormuz war, the Gulf is inside the blast radius.

Modelled as the obvious oil winner. In reality Gulf producers gained just 3.3% — because their own export infrastructure was struck and they could not ship. They win in someone else's oil war. Not in this one.

was   gulf_producers · oil +0.60  → +32.1%
now   gulf_producers · oil +0.15, gdp −0.15  → +4.9%
Correction 04 · Semiconductors

Sometimes the honest beta is zero.

Semis fell 8.2% in Ukraine and rose 11.4% in the Hormuz war. The two events disagree on the sign. That is not a signal to be tuned — it means semiconductors trade on the AI cycle, not on Middle East geopolitics.

The beta is set near zero and flagged low-confidence, rather than fitting a number to noise.

And one the old model got right

Airlines really do lose.

Ukraine had said otherwise — airlines beat the market in 2022, because post-COVID travel demand swamped the fuel bill. The Hormuz war settled it: aviation fell 9.9% below market, jet fuel rose about 95%, and Spirit Airlines ceased operations on 2 May 2026 citing fuel costs. The direction was right. The magnitude was 3.6× too harsh.

05 · The limits

What we are not claiming

One war is one sample.

The 2026 war ran six weeks to a ceasefire. A longer closure could transmit through corporate earnings in ways a six-week shock never did. These betas describe what has happened. They do not describe the tail that hasn't.

Shipping is the channel we measure worst — and it matters most.

Hormuz hit shipping hardest: tanker traffic fell to almost nothing and war-risk premiums spiked. There is no free tanker-rate index. Shipping, global GDP and defense appropriations all have no clean high-frequency series, so their stress levels are judgment, not measurement. We would rather state that than dress an estimate up as data.

The currency channel is the weakest link.

The emerging-market currency basket moved 1.5% during the Hormuz war and 0.7% during Ukraine. In both cases exporters offset importers, so there is almost no measured response to fit to. The currency betas are small and are the first thing we will revisit when a better event arrives.

Inflation betas are still judgment.

Monthly CPI gives roughly four data points inside a shock window. That is not enough to fit anything honestly, and we did not pretend otherwise.

Why this page exists

An exposure score you cannot interrogate is a number taken on faith. This one can be checked. The war is public. The tickers are liquid. The windows are stated above. The method is one line of arithmetic — sector return minus market return.

And the first thing this page told you is that we got it wrong the first time, by a factor of two to three, across the whole model.

If you think a beta is still wrong, you now have everything you need to argue with us.
