What the Exposure Score is doing

Every sector on the Exposure Desk carries a beta for each risk channel — a number describing how much that sector moves when oil, shipping, food, defense spending, global growth, or emerging-market currencies come under stress. Your score is your holdings, weighted, run through those betas.

So the betas are the whole model. If they are guesses, the score is a guess. They are not guesses.

01 · The evidence

Two conflicts, six years of weekly prices

Sector betas were fitted against 41 tickers of weekly closing prices running January 2020 through July 2026, across two historical analogues:

Ukraine is the fitting event: a genuine multi-channel shock where oil, food, defense budgets and global growth all moved at once. WTI went from a $77 pre-war average to a $124 peak.

The Red Sea is the control. It was a shipping-only event — oil barely registered, stress of roughly 0.01 on our scale. If our betas were over-firing, oil-sensitive sectors would have lit up anyway. They didn't. That is the test that matters, and it's the reason a second, quieter event was worth as much as the loud one.

02 · The method

One choice carries everything

The rule

Every return is measured net of the S&P 500 over the same window.

Sector return minus market return. That's the whole arithmetic.

The first half of 2022 was not only a war. It was the fastest Federal Reserve hiking cycle in four decades. Growth stocks fell hard in that window and most of that fall was about interest rates, not Ukraine.

A naive calibration absorbs the rate shock into the war beta and produces a model that screams whenever anything happens. Netting each sector against the broad market strips out what the whole market was doing anyway, and leaves the part that belongs to the conflict.

04 · What broke

Two betas were wrong. The data corrected them.

Correction 01 · Aviation

Airlines did not get crushed by expensive oil.

The draft model assumed the obvious: oil spikes, jet fuel costs explode, airlines crater. It carried a beta of −0.90 — near the maximum the model allows.

In 2022, airlines beat the market. Delta finished the invasion window +4.4% over the S&P; United +3.5%. Fuel did get far more expensive, and it didn't matter, because post-COVID travel demand was surging at exactly the same time.

was   aviation · oil −0.90   → predicts −59.6%
now   aviation · oil −0.55   → predicts −40.4%

Fuel is a real cost channel, so the beta stays negative. But it was nearly halved. This was the largest error in the draft model.

Correction 02 · Emerging markets

The sector average was hiding two opposite stories.

EM commodity exporters rallied in 2022 — Brazil gained 21.3% over the market. EM importers fell. Averaging them produced a number that described neither.

was   em_equity · fx −0.70
now   em_equity · fx −0.50   + oil channel added

The importer-pain thesis is retained, because a Hormuz event skews importer-heavy in a way Ukraine did not. But it is tempered, and this remains the weakest-evidenced sector in the model.

Correction 03 · Mislabelled funds

Two commodity funds were being scored as safe havens.

DBC and GSG sat in the gold bucket. They are broad commodity trackers. In 2022 they moved with the oil complex — +41.5% and +48.2% over market — not with the fear bid that lifted gold (+14.4%).

They now score off the oil channel, which is what they actually track.

05 · The limits

What we are not claiming

These are analogues, not replays.

A Strait of Hormuz event would be more oil- and shipping-intensive than Ukraine was, and would hit energy-importing emerging markets harder. The betas are fitted to the closest real evidence available. They are not a forecast of a specific future.

The currency channel is the weakest link.

The EM currency basket moved less than 1% during the 2022 invasion, because exporters and importers cancelled each other out. Our EM betas rest more on economic reasoning than on a clean measured response. We would rather say that than present false precision.

Shipping has no free rate index.

The obvious instrument — the dry-bulk freight ETF — fell 21% during Ukraine while tanker equities gained 51%. Wrong ship, wrong cargo. The shipping betas were validated through the Red Sea event instead, where the model predicted +7.7% for tankers and the market delivered +5.3%.

Inflation betas are still judgment.

Monthly CPI gives roughly four data points inside a shock window. That is not enough to fit anything honestly, and we did not pretend otherwise.

Why this page exists

An exposure score you cannot interrogate is a number taken on faith. This one can be checked. The events are public. The tickers are liquid. The windows are stated above. The method is one line of arithmetic.

If you think a beta is wrong, you now have everything you need to argue with us.
